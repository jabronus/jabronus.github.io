Biblical Foundation & Description

Jesus (n.) said, “Come (v.), follow (v.) me (n.)…” At once they (n.) left (v.) their nets and followed (v.) him (n.).

I nearly flunked English grammar. Ask Mrs. Harding, my 7th grade grammar teacher and she’ll validate that. But this much I know: The word Christian is a noun. A Christian is a person who follows Jesus. And I’ve learned over the years that nouns need verbs.

Jesus loved verbs. Verbs like die, live, come, rest, learn, hear, give, and go. So wouldn’t it make sense that the people who claim to follow Jesus would love the same verbs Jesus loved?

Somewhere along the way someone tried to make the word Christian an adjective. So now people speak of christian (adj.) books and christian (adj.) music and christian (adj.) t-shirts and christian (adj.) schools. When people use the word Christian as an adjective, instead of a noun that loves verbs, it loses its meaning.

Maybe this explains why so many churches have lost their meaning too.

If a Christian is a person (singular) who follows Jesus than the Church is a people (plural) who follow Jesus. And like a Christian, the Church is a noun too; a noun that was designed to love verbs. Specifically, a noun that loves the same verbs Jesus loved because the Church is a picture of Jesus.

I don’t mean this as an indictment but it seems as though some of us have lost our verbs.

When we lose our verbs we become what the world claims we are: hypocrites. A hypocrite is nothing more than a noun without a verb. Lovers that don’t love…givers that don’t give…followers that don’t follow…

In Nouns Need Verbs I want to paint a simple picture of a simple person (Christian) and a simple people (Church) who love the verbs Jesus loved.

Preface

Have you noticed the adjectives we put in front of the noun Christian?

“She’s such a loving Christian.”

“He’s such a giving Christian.”

Loving. Giving.

Is there any other kind of Christian? Can a Christian be a Christian without loving and giving? If a Christian doesn’t love or doesn’t give is that person really a Christian? Maybe the better way to ask the question is can a Christian be un-loving or un-giving?

Or have you noticed the adjectives we put in front of the noun Church?

The Baptist Church.

The Lutheran Church.

“It’s a traditional Church.”

“It’s a contemporary Church.”

“That’s a black Church.”

“That’s a white Church.”

The word that appears before the word Church in the New Testament more than any other word is the word the.

The Church.

The Church at Philippi…The Church at Thessalonica…The Church at Ephesus…

I like the word Jesus put in front of the word Church. Jesus told Peter, “The gates of hell will not prevail against MY church.”

I really like that.

HIS Church.

In the same way that the Church belongs to Jesus, I belong to Jesus. And you belong to Jesus.

WE belong to Jesus.

He is THE leader.

We follow (v.) him (n.)

When we follow Jesus, we don’t need adjectives to describe us.

I am a Christian.

We are the Church.

Period.

When we follow Jesus we will love like Jesus loves. When we follow Jesus we will give like Jesus gives.

So it’s not the word before the noun. It’s the word after the noun that we need to pay attention to.

Introduction: jump (v.)

When I was in the 4th grade I was invited to a birthday party at Chris’ house. I was excited because it was the first birthday party I had been to where girls had also been invited.

Specifically, Sarah Grossnickle had been invited.

Her last name is a bit unfortunate. But she had braces. I don’t know why I was so enamored with braces as a kid, but I was. Maybe the thought of having rubber bands in my mouth seemed so handy to me. Or maybe it was the thought of having a girl in my class with straight teeth someday.

I just know I was head over heels in love with Sarah Grossnickle.

I sent her several notes in class with a box to check. Yes. No. Maybe. On two separate occasions she checked the box that said Maybe. I was about to give her a reason to check the box that said Yes.

Everyone in my class was standing on the deck in Chris’ back yard. His parents were putting in a pool and had dug a huge hole and there was a huge pile of dirt about 10 feet from the deck. Well as 10-year-old boys do, a dare was thrown out.

“Who wants to play Follow the Leader?”

I was eating a box of Junior Mints when I accepted the dare. (It was easily my third or fourth box.) I wanted to set the bar high - so high no one would be able to follow me. So I marked off my steps. The drum roll began. And Sarah smiled at me, which was all the motivation I needed.

I could actually hear the song You’re the Inspiration by Chicago playing in my mind as I took off running!

With a lot of speed and momentum I planted my right foot on the railing of the deck to jump. However, the railing gave way.

Instead of going up, I went straight down.

Fortunately for me, a nail caught the edge of my pants slowing me down. Unfortunately for me, that nail ripped my pants completely off and I went sailing head first into a pile of bricks stacked neatly below the deck.

When I came to, I was sitting in the back of a Cadillac with leather interior in my Fruit of the Looms. And let’s just say the ‘ol Junior Mints were not agreeing with me. The only tactful way for me to put this is to say I “relinquished ownership” of the Junior Mints! And there wasn’t a product on the market that could clean up the mess I created in the backseat of that luxury automobile. And it wasn’t the last emergency room staff I would get acquainted with in life.

No one followed me that day. I was the only one that day who jumped.

Somewhere between that deck and the desk where I’m sitting right now, I lost the courage to take risks. Some might blame it on maturity. Others might point the finger at intelligence. I think the more birthdays we have, the fewer verbs we have. Our verb vocabulary shrinks with time.

Somewhere along the way I went to college, got married, had two kids, got a grown-up job, bought a house, started a 401K and somehow ended up driving a mini-van. (I’m still not sure how that one happened!)

In the midst of the “have to” it’s easy to lose sight of the “get to.”

I get to follow Jesus.

He is THE leader. He is MY leader. He is YOUR leader. He is OUR leader.

We get to follow Jesus. What a privilege!

And when we follow Jesus he doesn’t lead us to places as much as he leads us to people. That’s why there is nothing safe about following Jesus. Following Jesus is simple, but it’s not safe.

So my prayer for you is simple, but not safe. So simple it’s a single word. It’s a verb.

Jump.

It’s a verb that is looking for a noun.

I think YOU are the missing noun.

Insert your name here: ________________ jumped today.

It may seem safe to stand on the deck with the rest of the people. But standing on decks is not safe. It’s boring.

So go for it.

Jump.

Set down whatever you are holding onto or whatever has a hold of you.

And jump.

Helen Keller once said “Life is either a daring experience or it’s nothing at all.”

Decide today that you are going to follow THE leader. Decide today that you are going to be on a first name basis with the Emergency Room staff in a hospital near you. Decide today that you are going to trade “what is” for “what could be…”

Jump.

Chapter 1: be (v.)

One evening after work I turned onto our street and as I was driving towards the cul-de-sac where our house sits, I saw a little boy, maybe 5 years of age, throw open his front door and burst out. It was the dead of winter and all he was wearing was his Superman underoos and a bath towel turned cape tied around his neck.

As I drove down the road, he ran parallel to me on the sidewalk and as I glanced out the passenger side window, he locked eyes with me and smiled! Did I mention that his mom was chasing him with a wooden spoon yelling, “Stop! Stop!”

It was like a Norman Rockwell painting set in motion.

The grin on his face was saying, “Free at last! Free at last! Thank God Almighty I’m free at last!”

Later in the spring I saw him buried in a mud puddle up to his neck. My kind of boy! A few weeks later I watched him jump his bike off a massive pile of dirt, wearing nothing but his skivvies, an oversized motorcycle helmet and a pair of winter gloves. It was like I was watching Evel Knievel jump the fountains at Caesar’s Palace again.

I told my wife Allison, “I have to meet this kid!”

The very next night I was at the neighborhood park with my two children and I saw him. He was swinging on the swings as high as they could possibly carry him. And as I walked towards him, he jumped at the height of the upswing. Arms flailing, legs kicking, all I heard was “Geronimo!” He hit the ground like a stuntman, rolled through the mulch and jumped to his feet like he was Jason Bourne.

I said, “Son, what is your name?”

He put his hands on his hips and with a super hero pose, he said, “My name is Christian.”

I nodded my head and said, “Oh how fitting.”

to be (v.) or not to be (v.)

A group of masked men recently broke into a department store in the middle of the night and left without anyone noticing.

The next morning, store managers opened the large building for business and business went on as usual for several hours.

Until one of the clerks realized that people were getting incredible deals. Deals that were too good to be true - a cashmere sweater for $6…a designer handbag for $9…a Swiss watch for $14.

The thieves left empty-handed, but not before changing most of the prices on most of the items in the store. Yes, the price tags had been switched and very few people had noticed.

We live in a culture where the same thing has happened. The values have been changed and very few people seem to have noticed.

The voice whispers, “You’re valuable if you’re pretty. You’re valuable if you make a lot of money. You’re valuable if you live in the right neighborhood and drive the right car and wear the right clothes.”

The price tags have been switched. But people aren’t getting a bargain. People are being fleeced and business can’t go on as usual because the cost is too high.

It explains why teenagers cut themselves. It explains why more and more middle-aged women lack the motivation to get out of bed in the morning. It explains why more and more men self-medicate with a bottle of alcohol or prescription pain killers. People either want to sleep through life or numb their way through it.

We’ve become a culture of human doings when we were designed to live as human beings.

And when we deny our design, we run the risk of destroying our design.

And the verb be is a verb that flows from the blueprints of our Designer.

God was. God is. And God will always…BE.

And God has invited you to “be” his child.

How great is the love the Father has lavished on us that we should be (v.) called the children of God. And that is what we are. - I John 3:1

When my wife Allison was 28 weeks pregnant with our first child, she called me to let me know that her feet were swollen. Water retention is not an uncommon occurrence in pregnancy. But just to be safe, she stopped at a drug store to check her blood pressure.

Sure enough the numbers were sky high.

So she called our doctor and he invited Allison to come in for a quick check-up. I met her at the hospital thinking it would be a routine visit.

I could tell from the look on his face that something wasn’t right. After running a few tests, he informed us that Allison would need to be on bed rest for the duration of her pregnancy. That was a curve ball that hit us from left field. Twelve weeks is a long time to be in bed. We were not expecting that news.

After running a series of other tests he informed us that Allison would have to stay in the hospital for the duration of her pregnancy. That was an even bigger blow. And less than an hour later, he sat down on the edge of Allison’s bed and I could see the sadness in his body language. He said, “I hate to tell you this, but it looks like you are going to deliver your baby within the night.”

As those words rolled out of his mouth a team of nurses came in the room and started to hook my wife up to all kinds of machines. It happened so fast. And the tears came just as quickly for us.

My prayers went from very generic prayers of, “God help us deal with this.” To very desperate and lonely cries of, “God I beg of you…please work a miracle on behalf of my wife and little girl.

I would be the first to tell you that I wasn’t questioning God. I wasn’t asking, “Why me? Why my wife? Why my firstborn child?” I’ve lived enough life in this fragile and fallen world to know this truth - God is good even when life isn’t good to me.

By morning, Allison’s physical condition had worsened and doctors feared we would lose her life if we didn’t deliver the life inside of her. I was tired of being tired. I was scared and worried, but strangely I was excited too. I was going to meet my daughter! I was going to be a dad!

15 minutes later Ava Joy was born - all 2 pounds of her - 3 months early.

At least 10 doctors discussed with Allison and I all the potential complications Ava would face in life - hearing loss, blindness, and a litany of other developmental challenges. Honestly, the list was longer than I could have imagined. But at the time, that list didn’t matter to me. I was a dad. And dads don’t care what their children can or can’t do. We love them! And the reason I loved Ava was because she was mine! I helped make her. Part of me was in her.

In the middle of the night, when no one else was around, I stared into this little glass tube where my little girl was fighting for her life. And it hit me - God had heard all the reports on me. And in spite of how grave they are. In spite of my spiritual pre-maturity, he loves me. Because in the purest, truest sense of the word, God is a dad. And part of him is in me. And part of him is in you.

I stood over Ava’s incubator and I wanted what was best for her. Whatever the cost, whatever the procedure, I pleaded with doctors to do what they could to help her live. And I read the Bible and it is a love letter from a lovesick dad to his homesick children. And in it he says, “Whatever the cost, whatever the procedure - even if it means giving my life for yours - I will do it, I will go through it for you!”

I stood over Ava’s incubator and I watched medical professionals cut my daughter, poke her, prod her and put her through the proverbial medical ringer. And through all of it, I didn’t want her to go through it alone. So I would sit by her bed. I would stand by her bed. I would eat and sleep by her bed. I wanted her to hear and know my voice. And I read the Bible and I hear the voice of my Father say, “Never will I leave you; never will I abandon you.”

I stood over Ava’s incubator and I was so proud. I used to sing Jesus loves you (Ava) this I know. For the Bible tells me so. And I would get to that part in the song that says Little ones to him belong. They are weak, but he is strong, and I would cry. My daughter was so small I could fit my wedding band over her thigh. I still can’t sing that song without crying because of Zephaniah’s words:

The Lord your God is with you, he is mighty to save. He will take great delight in you, he will quiet you with his love, he will rejoice over you with singing.

You have a Dad who sings over you. He’s so proud of you. The Bible teaches that he has your name written on the palm of his hand - the same hand that took a nail for you.

He didn’t go through that pain so that you would spend your entire life questioning whether or not he really loves you. He REALLY loves you.

And before you can BE his follower, you have to BE His child.

Let’s start there.

Chapter 2: love (v.)

It was a Saturday morning. When I lived in Haiti that was the morning I went to the open-air market to buy my food for the week.

I was standing in the middle of the street holding a bag filled with oranges and rice and beans. And that is when I saw him.

It was like a page had been torn out of a National Geographic magazine and framed for me to stare at.

He was crippled. His legs were disproportionate in size to the rest of his body and they were bent at an angle that was unfamiliar to a healthy torso. His head was bowed in shame, but I could tell he was blind. His eyes never really fixed on any particular object rather they danced from side to side and followed the noises around him. He was barely clothed and his body was caked with dust and dirt. His hair was matted down from sweat and flies took up residence on his skin.

His hands were outstretched. He was holding a small bowl. He was begging.

I watched as one person after another passed by him…ignored him…pretended he didn’t exist. It seems easier that way.

Then he pulled a small board from his bag and placed it in the sewage that runs beside the road. Backing it’s flow up, he did something I wish I could scrub from conscience. He placed his hands in the raw sewage and began to sift and search as if he was looking for something of value. Living in Haiti I watched a woman drink from a mud puddle and I caught two men eating my garbage, but this was a new low.

My heart broke and scattered into pieces I will not recover this side of heaven.

I watched as he pulled out an old battery. He cleaned it off like it was it was a rare artifact missing from a prestigious museum.

With tears streaming down my cheeks, I knelt down in front of him and took his dirty hands and placed them on my face and in Kreyol said, “My name is Jon and I want to be your friend.”

He said, “My parents broke my legs when I was a baby so that I could beg and bring in money for our family.”

I filled his empty bag with my bag full of food. I filled his pocket with my pocket full of money.

Love empties itself, because Love emptied Himself.

Jesus asked a blind man named Bartimaeus, “What do you want me to do for you?”

That’s always the question that love asks.

What can I do for you?

I have a friend named Donnie. When Donnie was 6 years old he watched his dad nearly beat his mom to death. The trauma of that episode locked Donnie into a permanent state of child-likeness. Though he is 52 years of age today, Donnie thinks and acts and communicates like a 6-year-old.

Donnie loves me and I love Donnie.

He has taught me a lot about love.

Donnie washed dishes at a local restaurant for two decades. Each Friday he would cash his paycheck and each Saturday he would ride his bike from one garage sale to the next buying albums and paper novels and costume jewelry.

Donnie has a Christmas gift list with 385 people on it. Donnie loves people and people love Donnie. So much so that he spends his entire year Christmas shopping for all the people he loves.

One of my prized possessions is a ring that looks like it came from a gumball machine. And maybe it did. I don’t really care WHERE it came from because I know WHO it came from.

I know it came from Donnie. I know it came from Donnie’s heart.

Donnie understands Christmas better than most people do.

Donnie couldn’t tell you WHERE Jesus came from. But Donnie does know WHO sent Jesus. And Donnie knows WHY the Father sent Jesus.

Love.

Donnie doesn’t know a stranger. When he meets people for the first time, he hugs them. And he doesn’t let go! When Donnie hugs people, he holds on to people! And it doesn’t matter who you are, once Donnie learns your name, your name finds its way on to his Christmas list. From the mayor of our city to the homeless men in Phoenix Park, Donnie knows a lot of people by name.

Donnie looks a lot like love.

Love holds on. Love gives. Love knows.

Donnie is on a fixed income. God isn’t.

God never runs out of anything.

The Bible teaches, “God is love.” Which means he never runs out of love. No matter how great the demand for love is, God is never in short supply.

And what I love about God is he doesn’t love things the way people do. God loves people.

More specifically, God loves you.

He wants to hold you, give to you, and know you. And He wants you to do the same for others.

John once wrote, Whoever does not love (v.) does not know God, because God is love.

John referred to himself as “the disciple whom Jesus loved.”

How do you refer to yourself?

If you don’t feel loved, it’s not because you aren’t loved.

One of the ways to get love is to give love.

Most Tuesdays I stand on a corner in the downtown district of my hometown with a sign that says “Free Hugs.” I’ve done it for over a decade now. One man looked at my sign for awhile, shrugged his shoulders in a disappointed kind of way and said, “I thought it said ‘Free Hogs’!”

Only in Kentucky would someone be sad that I wasn’t giving away free farm animals!

My favorite street corner is across the street from the courthouse. As part of their sentence for breaking the law, a local judge sends those he convicts to me and as part of their punishment they have to hug me!

I get a lot of “side” hugs from the men.

One lady rode up on a bike not too long ago and said, “What does your sign say?” She couldn’t read. So I told her what it said. She cocked her head to the side and took another drag on the cigarette she was smoking. Then she squinted her eyes and said, “Are you for real?”

I said, “Come a little closer and find out.”

So she got off her bike and I went in for the kill.

When I tried to pull away from her she pulled me in tighter. She buried her head in my chest and said, “Nobody’s hugged me in a long time.”

When nobody hugs you, you begin to feel like a nobody.

Nobody is a nobody to God. Everybody is a somebody to God.

And one of the ways we experience His love for us is by giving his love to others.

And there’s no way to give love away without getting closer.

God came down and love came with Him.

God didn’t try to love us at a distance. And God doesn’t stiff-arm anyone who needs to be loved.

God doesn’t give side hugs.

Chapter 3: see (v.)

My dad died of cancer.

I hate cancer.

I love my dad.

Although the cancer ravaged his mouth and throat, it did not lay hold of his mind and his heart, nor did it blur his vision.

One afternoon I was sitting beside the chair he was resting in feeding him ice chips and I noticed his eyes were moving around a lot. He smiled several times, so finally I looked around the family room and asked, “Dad what do you see?”

He could only whisper so I leaned in and he said, “You don’t see them?”

I looked around the room again. From my perspective the room was empty. So I said, “No dad, I don’t see THEM. What do you see?”

He pulled me close and said, “Son, there are children everywhere.”

We both started to cry.

I asked, “Tell me dad, what do THEY look like?”

In spite of the pain, he smiled from ear to ear and said, “Son THEY’RE so beautiful and so full of life.”

A few hours later and just a few hours away from his death, he looked past all of us seated at the table and fixed his eyes on my mom who was washing dishes at the kitchen sink. And he said to my mom, “Carol do you feel that?”

She turned off the water, dried her hands and said, “Feel what?”

He said, “The two hands in the middle of your back.”

My siblings and I stopped what we were doing as he smiled at my mom and said, “He’s holding you…he knows you’re weak, he knows you’re tired, and he just wants you to know that he’s strong enough for both of you.”

Jesus said, “The pure in heart will see God.”

A holy heart is required to see a holy God.

New York City has more nearsighted people than any other city in the U.S. Ophthalmologists believe the phenomenon is due to residents using their eyes repeatedly for short distances due to tall buildings.

Too often spiritual nearsightedness limits what I see and how far I can see.

And more importantly WHO I see.

Afterwards I had my students raise their heads from their desk and one little girl who shared a bed with 5 siblings each night, asked, “Mr. Jon, will I have my own bed in heaven?”

My response? “Absolutely!”

Another little girl who came to school undernourished asked, “Will we get to eat whatever we want in heaven?”

My response? “Absolutely!”

And one little boy in the back whose dad drown and whose mom died in a fire said, “I want to see him! I want to see Jesus!”

My response? “Me too.”

I want to see Jesus.

Chapter 6: dance (v.)

My cousin Kathy was born with Downs Syndrome.

Every year when our family would gather at my grandparent’s house in western Kansas, Kathy would set up all of the birthday cards she had received the previous year on the kitchen table and explain why each one meant so much to her.

She also loved to show us the engagement ring that her “boyfriend” had given her. The wedding wasn’t scheduled for a specific date on the calendar rather each year she would tell us, “I’m getting married in 14 years!” My two older brothers were going to be ushers. I was assigned the distinct role of ring bearer. But as each year passed I explained to her that if I had to wait 14 more years, I might just be the oldest ring bearer in human history! Kathy would laugh and give me one of her signature hugs.

Hugging Kathy made me realize how beautiful life is.

Kathy added beauty to this life for 48 years.

I miss hugging Kathy. It was a privilege. But what I really miss is Kathy hugging me.

Someday you’ll get to experience a hug from Kathy. I can’t imagine heaven would be complete without her welcoming people to the party.

And that’s ultimately what this idea is about.

A party. The party.

Where I’m from we call it Prom Night.

I wish I could remember my prom night. It was such a blur, a hurried mess of getting dressed, taking pictures, eating out and hanging out that I really don’t remember much of it. I guess it was too much like every other prom so I’ve chosen to forget it.

But I haven’t forgotten the second prom I went to. I will always remember it.

And the reason I will always remember it is because all of the people who were never invited to a High School prom were invited to this one.

Beautiful people like my cousin Kathy.

Beautiful people like my friend Audrey.

I pay the bills by standing on a stage each weekend and teaching thousands of people to love people. It’s a rewarding gig.

Audrey sits on the front row each weekend and after listening to me for 10 minutes she usually gets up and goes to the bathroom. It’s also a very humbling gig.

During one of my talks Audrey stood up, but instead of walking to the back of the room where the bathrooms are located, she turned to the crowd and motioned for all of them to stand up too. Trust me, I hadn’t said anything profound. Audrey just felt led to give me a standing ovation. She started to clap and cheer and when no one else joined her, she turned around a second time and put both hands on her hips as if to say, “I mean it people! Get up off you’re lazy can and give this man a round of applause!”

Three seconds later I received a standing ovation from 3,000 people.

I thanked Audrey and she said, “You’re welcome Jon!”

Jesus said, “When you throw a party, don’t invite your friends, brothers, relatives or rich neighbors, for they will repay you by inviting you to their party. Instead, when you throw a party invite the poor, the crippled, the lame, and the blind.”

We decided to throw a party for Audrey and her friends.

So we sent out an invitation to physically and mentally challenged people living in our city and we asked them to come to prom with us.

We invited local tuxedo stores and formal dress stores to provide free attire for our guests to wear. And they did!

We invited local restaurants to cater the event. And they did!

We invited local limousine owners to pick up and deliver our privileged friends. And they did!

We invited tailors and seamstresses to make sure every article of clothing fit like it had been custom made for a runway in SOHO. Whether they were wheel chair bound, bent over, or missing a leg - who better to be adorned in the finest of clothing!

We rolled out the red carpet and we handed hundreds of disposable cameras to Middle School students who played the role of the paparazzi. We hung disco balls and strobe lights and put a live band on stage to cover the best Motown has to offer.

And as thousands of people poured in something magical happened in the universe. A new normal was defined. A new flavor of love was showcased. A new picture of perfection was captured.

If Jesus were anywhere on the planet that night, he would have been in that room on that dance floor cutting a rug with the first class citizens of Lexington, Kentucky.

Someone more perceptive than me once observed, “Grace is the face love wears when it meets imperfection.”

The many people who have put up with me, not given up on me, included me, and helped me, have all looked at me through the lens of grace.

Prom night has changed how I look at people.

Prom night has changed how I look at me.

Prom night has changed how I look at love.

Silence.

I love Andrew.

When Charlie’s mom went to the hospital to deliver him, her doctor was at a Christmas party. He showed up in the delivery room intoxicated and angry.

He was angry because he had to leave the party.

So he pushed on her stomach. It was her first pregnancy so she didn’t know the protocol. She assumed his behavior was standard procedure. But standard medical procedure doesn’t take the life of a young mom.

Not only did Charlie never meet the mom who gave him life, Charlie did not receive enough oxygen in the process and was born with Cerebral Palsy.

By the time I was in high school, the first two rows of my home church were filled with Charlie and his friends.

Hearing Charlie sing, Oh How I Love Jesus, was one of the highlights of my life. I know Jesus loves Charlie. And I know Charlie loves Jesus.

Everybody’s odd but God.

Chapter 10: remember (v.)

The Vietnam Veteran’s Memorial in Washington D.C. is striking for its simplicity.

Etched in a black granite wall are the names of 58,156 Americans who died in the Vietnam War. Since its opening in 1982, that stark monument has stirred deep emotions.

Some visitors walk its length slowly, almost reverently without pause. Others stop before certain names, remembering their son or fianc or fellow soldier, wiping away tears, tracing the names with their fingers.

For three Vietnam veterans - Robert Bedker, Willard Craig, and Darrall Lausch - a visit to the memorial must be especially poignant, for they can walk up to the long ebony wall and find their own names carved in the stone.

Because of a clerical error, each of them was incorrectly listed as killed in action; they were thought to be dead, when really, they are alive.

In my mind I make the journey up the hill to Golgotha every day. It’s a war memorial I need to visit.

I do not want to forget the cross.

Rembrandt painted a picture of the cross. His rendering is really no different than most Renaissance paintings - a hill, three crosses, a dark sky, and a crowd.

What separates Rembrandt’s painting from other depictions of the crucifixion is a man who stands in the crowd dressed in 17th century clothing.

He stands out from the other observers.

Rembrandt knew enough to paint himself into the picture of the cross.

I don’t want to forget the cross.

So I run my hand over the contours of the wood. And with each return visit I find my name etched in the bloodstained grain. And I remember, “I was once dead, but now I am alive.”

And the good news is that’s not a clerical error.

That’s not good news. That’s great news!

X marks the spot.

God is strong.

In 1995 I climbed in a car with some friends from college and we drove to the site of the Oklahoma City bombing. We parked our car several blocks away and I remember how quiet it was as we walked toward the epicenter of the blast.

Epilogue: add-verbs

Don’t just serve. Don’t just give. Don’t just love.

Serve joyfully. Give sacrificially. Love recklessly.

It’s amazing what can happen to your life when you add adverbs. Adverbs add so much to verbs.

Adverbs like radically, outrageously, spontaneously, abundantly, frequently, honestly, differently, beautifully, secretly, courageously, and generously.

It’s amazing what “LY” can do to the quality of your life.

When I read how the people of the Bible lived the word faithfully comes to mind.

When I look at how most people today are living their lives the word pathetically comes to mind.

It’s a choice. How you live is a choice.

So how are you going to live?

Sadly? Boringly? Indecisively? Reluctantly? Fearfully?

If you live like that, you are not really living.

How we live will determine how people see Jesus.

So let me end where I started.

Jump.

And don’t just jump.

Add an adverb.

If it were up to me I would tell you to…

…jump quickly.
